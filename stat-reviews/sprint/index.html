Review and Statistical Critique of the SPRINT Trial

Reviewer: Robert Long
Position: Principal Consultant
Company: The Data Guru Ltd
Date: 18 May 2025
Document Version: 1.5

I. Introduction & Background

The SPRINT trial, published by Wright et al. (2015), was a landmark randomised controlled trial (RCT) investigating whether systolic blood pressure (SBP) targets for high-risk, non-diabetic patients should be lowered to <120 mm Hg rather than the traditional <140 mm Hg.

At the time, international guidelines varied considerably, driven by inconclusive evidence from earlier trials such as ACCORD-BP (2010) and HYVET (2008). SPRINT influenced guidelines in the United States, recommending stricter SBP control (<130 mm Hg).

This review critically evaluates the trial’s statistical methods, design, outcomes, and implications. It draws upon primary and ancillary trial publications, meta-analyses, and key reappraisals, notably Wright et al. (2021), who address several methodological criticisms.

II. Trial Design and Methodology

SPRINT was a publicly funded, multi-centre, open-label RCT with blinded outcome adjudication. It was conducted at 102 clinical sites in the US and enrolled 9,361 participants aged ≥50 years at elevated cardiovascular risk. Key exclusions included diabetes mellitus and prior stroke.

Randomised groups:

  • Intensive treatment: SBP target <120 mm Hg
  • Standard treatment: SBP target <140 mm Hg

Blood pressure was measured using the Omron HEM-907XL device. Most readings were taken unattended to reduce white-coat effects.

Inclusion Criteria

  • Age ≥50 years
  • SBP 130–180 mm Hg
  • At least one CV risk factor (e.g., CVD, CKD, age ≥75, Framingham 10-year risk ≥15%)

Exclusion Criteria

  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Prior stroke
  • LVEF <35% or symptomatic heart failure
  • Significant proteinuria

The trial was stopped early (median follow-up: 3.26 years) due to observed benefit.

III. Main Findings and Outcomes

Primary composite outcome:

  • HR = 0.75, 95% CI [0.64, 0.89], p < 0.001

All-cause mortality:

  • HR = 0.73, 95% CI [0.60, 0.90], p = 0.004

Secondary Outcomes

  • Reduced heart failure and CV death
  • No significant difference in stroke
  • More incident CKD (reversible)
  • Mild cognitive impairment reduced, but no effect on dementia

IV. Critical Appraisal

Internal Validity

  • Randomisation and blinding robust
  • Outcomes adjudicated blind to group
  • Early stopping may overstate effect size

External Validity

  • Exclusions reduce generalisability (no diabetes, prior stroke)
  • Low representation of frail/elderly

Composite Outcome Bias

Effects were mainly driven by heart failure and CV death, not evenly across all endpoints.

Harms

  • Increased adverse events: hypotension, syncope, AKI

Blood Pressure Measurement

Unattended measurements may not reflect routine clinical care.

V. Clinical and Policy Implications

  • US guidelines now favour SBP <130 mm Hg in high-risk patients
  • European/UK guidelines remain cautious
  • Implementation requires careful measurement and individualised plans

VI. Conclusions

SPRINT was robust and influential. Its results support lower SBP targets for selected patients, but generalisability and risk–benefit trade-offs must be carefully considered in clinical implementation.
